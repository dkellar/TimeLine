March 13
Covid 19

Maracaibo in 2020: A Year That Shifted Everything In early March 2020, we sent out a regular newsletter celebrating Maracaibo’s 50th anniversary, sharing updates on cottage rentals and ongoing community business. Within days, everything changed. As the Covid‑19 pandemic advanced, operations across the island—and around the world—came to an abrupt halt. Immediate Impacts Cottage bookings were cancelled almost overnight. Members already staying in cottages chose to extend their stays as travel restrictions tightened. Board meetings moved to Zoom and were held every couple of weeks to keep pace with the rapidly evolving situation. Keeping Maracaibo Running Despite the uncertainty, Maracaibo staff were able to continue working, ensuring essential operations remained stable. Projects already underway moved forward, though supply delays became a regular challenge. Welcoming Guests Back—Carefully By summer, we were able to welcome Canadian members and guests back to the cottages under strict Covid protocols. Stays were shortened to allow for additional cleaning and distancing. Sadly, our international members were unable to travel and were deeply missed. Staying Connected Community connection took on new forms: A few virtual happy hours on Zoom brought familiar faces together during the spring. Summer happy hours shifted outdoors to the meadow, with a fully BYOE approach—bring your own everything.

December 8
Directors Meeting Highlights

The December Directors meeting is always an eye crossing event as the financial statements and proposed budgets and capital expenditures are reviewed in detail. This year we added a new process to determine a more business-like allocation of charges for the services that Maracaibo Estates Ltd charges the Strata Corporation for its maintenance contract. Increases in dues and assessments are in part due to: A compensation agreement with the Bazzards to provide on-going training throughout 2013 to ensure a smooth transition for Pete and Victoria. The Strata Corporation is paying higher annual contract fees to Maracaibo Estates Ltd. ($89,174 for 2013 was $81,120 in 2012). Another change is increased allowance for depreciation on the Main House which will come into effect part way into 2013. HST is going in April and being replaced by GST and PST. Once the change is implemented Maracaibo will only charge GST on its services and dues. PST will not be charged or claimed. Dues and Assessments for 2013 were: Maracaibo Estates Ltd – semi-annual dues will be $772.50 (2012 was $750.00). Strata Corp 905 – semi-annual assessment will be $775.00 (2012 was $700).

March 18
Winter storms in Long Harbour

We had a few anxious days because of storms this winter. As well as bringing down trees and subsequent power outages, the first winds broke loose a boat that was being renovated at anchor out towards the ferry dock. This fifty+-footer was washed onto the beach by the Easter Meadow where it sunk. Three cats were rescued from the boat, and Charles spent a couple of nights at low tide helping the search and rescue team to see if the holes in the boat could be plugged. We were much relieved when the owners finally had it removed by a tug using a couple of large pumps to keep it afloat. A few weeks later another large motorboat came loose from its mooring near our dock and collided with a catamaran that was swept to the extreme end of the inlet. The big boat then became entangled with a small sailboat. We towed it to our dock once the weather died down to keep it from further mischief and it is now safely back on a new mooring buoy – a hard lesson in the need to check anchor chains!

Non Potable Water

Water from the works yard well is still smelly. The groundwater geologist recommends we continue to pump water from the well to help remediate the problem. An old concrete water storage tank above the Core Area was patched and a roof installed. Waterlines were then connected from the well to the tank and water gravity fed to irrigate the gardens in the Core Area and to be used for boat and car washing. This worked very well, the smell from the water dissipated as it aerated going into the water tank, and after 10 more years of use the smell problem decreased sufficiently to allow the water to be used as potable water in the main supply system. July 6
Water system smelly water

A problem developed with the water quality from the well in the worksyard. The water was smelly and had traces of hydrocarbons. It was a complicated issue, the well being located in a shale bed with bacteria producing hydrocarbons from the ancient vegetation making up the shale. Another complication was a fuel spill in the worksyard and a high level of methane gas. Eventually the problem was solved by letting the well rest and then pumping to waste and using the water for irrigation. Some 30 years later the well is clear and back in use.

Seawall

Andrew had been bought over from Scotland to do the stonework at the Hastings House Hotelrenovations. A true craftsmen he trained and influenced most of the masons on the Island. During the construction Charles recalls the following incident. “I had carried a partiularly large rock in the front bucket of the backhoe to the site of the sea wall but needed to lift it into place with the back bucket. The rock was so heavy that as I lifted it the front wheels of the machine came off the ground and I was unable to steer. In order to counter balance the rock Andrew and his crew, including a huge man called Billy Two Feathers, climbed into the front bucket. I was now able to manaoever. On approaching the seawall, we found the wheelbarrow in the way. Billy Two feathers hopped out of the front bucket to move the barrow, but the lack of his weight caused the front bucket to lift again! The back went down and the tongs holding the rock released. The front bucket was now heavier than the back so descended at great speed. Luckily no one was injured and a good laugh had by all!

November 5
Oyster Business Sold

Maracaibo is very busy and the income from the oyster business does not justify the time spent on the operation. At the height of the business we were selling 60 dozen oysters a week but to expand we were required to build a packing plant, a ridiculous requirement as we only sold live oysters in the shell. We sold oysters to Neptune Foods in Victoria, Coopers Cove Oyster Farm in Sooke, at the Farmers Market in Ganges and to the Harbour House Hotel. All the stock and equipment were sold to an oyster farm setting up on Saturna Island. It was an interesting venture, we grew excellent oysters fast, but the marine fouling (barnacles especially) was very heavy and providing clean oysters for the restaurant market was very labour intensive. We managed to clear our costs but did not get adequate reward for time. We also experimented with growing mussels in mesh ‘socks’ suspended from a raft. Charles collected mussels from rocks in Active Pass and they grew very succesfully until the ducks and otters found them!

Jim and Finbar were fixing her up to tow north as a fishing camp or packer. The Cy Peck was a great source of entertainment and angst for the Long Harbour residents. The paint job which was dependant an cheap supplies or donations, was pink one side and green the other! One stormy day she was careening around at her moorings and one end broke free. We managed to secure a line to a mooring bouy and she rode out the storm.

September 6
# 4 Cottage Approved For Construction

Dogwood Cottage approved for construction. The architect was Barry Griblin (Bob Hassell’s partner), contractor Eddy Jang, construction contract price $39,445. Claire Pickering and Graham Clay were on the building committee and very involved in choosing the furnishings. The cottage was completed in time for the 1978 summer season. This first Dogwood cottage, (also known at #4) and now removed to develop Core Area housing, was beautifully sited with a lovely outlook down Long Harbour amongst the arbutus trees. The living room with its window seats was a wonderful social space and suitable for large gatherings of family and friends.

September 1
Strata Development Proposal

In 1977 the Province of B.C. was developing regulations to allow development of bare land in a similar way to as condominium ownership. This opened the door to a style of development appropriate for the Members at Maracaibo. A meeting of Members was held at the Planetarium in Vancouver. Bob Hassell led a presentation showing development options for the whole property. Some Members expressed concern that such a beautiful property should be developed. Bob took those concerns into consideration andthis led to the development of a Master Plan that was presented in 1978.

August 6
Sea Wall Collapse

Seawall on west side of the Main House collapses due to high tide and erosion. A new wall had to be build very quickly to save the house footings from erosion. The next low tide we excavated and prepared forms for a wall fitting. The next day we poured the footings using a pumper truck to reach over the house, and day after the wall was poured. The weather was extremely hot and the concrete had additives to speed up curing to set ahead of the incoming tide. We had to move fast!

May 6
Clamshell Cottage Completed

When Clamshell was completed, It was pretty basic. Access was by a narrow gravel road . There was no water or eletrical power. Propane was installed for lights and cooking, a wood stove for heat, and an outhouse nearby. Water had to be carried in. Hence the cottage was often called the “Wilderness Cottage”.

Clamshell Cottage

Sally Bazzard who was working for the B.C. assessment Authority advised that the property tax on District Lot 10 (250 acres) could be halved if an improvement was built on the lot. Charles designed a simple A frame cottage based on a magazine drawing. Charles Bazzard and Mike Spencer started construction in December and had the site cleared and footings poured before the end of the year. September 29
Pickleball Courts

In 1970 Jack Russell proposed building a tennis court at a cost of between $3,500 – $5,000 but a site could not be agreed upon. The Beckmann family had a Pickle Ball court at their home in Vancouver and in 1976. Quentin Brown was easily convinced that Pickleball was a great game, that the footprint required was a quarter of the size of a tennis court and so construction was approved by the Advisory committee. Charles Bazzard designed the net posts so they could to be raised to allow badminton to be played as the courts as they are the same size. Pickleball has proved a great success over the years.

July 29
Laser Club

Quentin Brown arranged for the purchase of two Laser sailboats. A club of 20 members each donating $100 towards purchase of the boats was formed. Membership was for a life. Annual maintenace fee started at $10. Over the years Membership in the club expanded and three more boats were purchased. Laser sailing was very popular up until the early 2000’s when interest in kayaking took over. Eventually the club was disbanded and all Maracaibo Members can use the boats at no additional charge. For a number of years Laser races were part of the Maracaibo Olympics events.

The 1976 Olympics were held in Montreal. The Williams family were spending a week in a cottage. Audrey smuggled a TV into the cottage so she could watch the games. The family were aghast and decided to organise their own Olympics as an alternative to watching TV. The Vogel and George Price family also competed. Charles Bazzard was asked to judge. May 22
Phase 1 District Lot 8

Decision made to subdivide a portion of District lot 8. Two of these lots were for the Russells’ homestead. Bob Hassell (planner) Charles Bazzard (site manager). The initial survey found the whole of District lot 8 land area was 17 acres larger than the previous survey! The area was zoned Rural with 5 acre lot average size permitted. It was decided to maximise the density to take advantage of higher values along the waterfront which resulted in 50 acres being subdivided into 19 lots with an average density of 2.63 acres per lot. As a result Phase II of D.L. 8 would have a reduced density (16 lots on 128 acres).

The Province recognizes the Gulf Islands as a unique area threatened by over-development and establishes the Islands Trust. The Islands Trust Act solidified the Province’s mandate to preserve and protect the environment and unique nature of the islands.

June 22
The 20

13 ft Sangster craft with 20 hp outboard (The 20) was purchased for Members use and evening beach patrols by the managers. During the 1970’s boaters would often anchor off the beaches and light campfires. These posed a serious fire risk and needed to be monitored. Charles remembers Tom Toynbee (a rowing champion) being easily able to haul the 20 onto the dock single handed. Charles’ and Sally’s combined strength could barely manage it. The 20 did great communal service until an outboard motor fell off due to some erratic driving and Members’ use was discontinued. In 1990 the 20 was replaced by the 14 ft Kellahan and sold to Henry Boudin. Picture shows Charles at the helm in 1976.

January 4
Moats agree sale to Maracaibo Syndicate

Charles and Mary Moat sold District lot 9 to the Maracaibo Syndicate in 1970. Charles and Mary had purchased this property in 1957 and called it Maracaibo. They had a great vision for the property and set about demolishing many of the old farm buildings and the original Long Harbour House. They made improvements to the Main House including adding a tropical conservatory. They dammed the bay to create a tidal lagoon, creating a swimming and boating area safe for children. They built three A frame cottages and had plans for a marina development. They felt the property should be enjoyed by more than one family. After the sale the Moats stayed on for a couple of years to manage the property.
